header: Demography, Early Voting, and Election Integrity in South Korea
This repository contains the replication code, data, and supplementary materials for the manuscript "Demography, Early Voting, and Election Integrity in South Korea: Evidence Across Four Electoral Cycles" by Minseong Kim. 

header: Overview
Following the introduction of nationwide early voting in South Korea in 2013, early ballots have consistently returned higher Democratic Party vote shares than same-day ballots. This divergence has sparked persistent allegations of systematic electoral fraud. This project assesses these claims using a unified empirical framework applied to four major elections spanning 2020 to 2025: the 21st and 22nd General Elections (2020, 2024) and the 20th and 21st Presidential Elections (2022, 2025).

header: Methodology
The research investigates the generative process behind early and same-day ballots using two complementary analytical strategies:
- Forensic Statistical Tests: Application of simulation-adjusted second-digit Benford's law, last-digit uniformity tests, mixture-model fingerprinting, and scrutiny of the widely circulated "63:37" early-vote ratio claim.
- Sociodemographic Regression Analysis: Population-weighted least squares regression at the sub-district (dong) level. This models vote shares using principal components derived from 51 census, employment, and housing variables. For the three-candidate 2025 presidential election, an instrumental-variable (IV-2SLS) approach uses the 2017 vote share of Yoo Seung-min to isolate the causal effect of third-party vote diversion.

header: Key Findings
- No Systemic Fraud: Forensic tests reveal no systematic pattern of large-scale ballot manipulation across the 11,436 dong-election observations in the four analyzed cycles.
- Demographics Explain the Gap: Sub-district sociodemographic characteristics explain 73% to 94% of the geographic variation in vote shares. The predictors perform equally well for both early votes and same-day votes, indicating that the early-voting divergence is driven by demographic sorting (who votes early) rather than counting manipulation.
- The "63:37" Claim is Unsupported: Early-voting two-party ratios vary substantially across different elections. While the ratio was approximately 63% in metropolitan areas in 2020, it dropped to 53%–59% in 2022, and rose to 72%–73.7% in 2025. Furthermore, the micro-level standard deviation across precincts is large (between 6.4 and 9.1 percentage points), refuting the existence of a fixed mathematical algorithm. 
- 2022 Anomaly: A candidate-specific last-digit anomaly was flagged in the 2022 presidential election. While it warrants further scrutiny, the magnitude of the deviation is small (Cramer's V = 0.028) and does not invalidate the broader demographic findings.

header: Repository Contents
In accordance with the manuscript's data availability statement, this repository provides the full forensic and regression results, alongside the necessary scripts and aggregated data to reproduce the findings.

The main regression file (run concurrently with the main forensic file): korean_election_regressions.ipynb
